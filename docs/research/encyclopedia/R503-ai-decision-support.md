## 1. Purpose

[R210](R210-decision-support-systems.md) supplies the general 4-level decision-support spectrum (data/information/recommendation/
decision); this topic applies that spectrum specifically to the concrete case DOM-008 §4 already
names — a future in-world AI assistant for the trainee — and works through what such a feature could
and could not legitimately do under the advisor-only constraint.

## 2. Scope

Covers: applying [R210](R210-decision-support-systems.md)'s general 4-level taxonomy specifically to a hypothetical in-world
trainee-facing advisor, and the additional pedagogical-preservation requirement (does the feature
leave a genuine judgment task for the trainee) that is specific to a *training* exercise rather than
an operational decision aid. Does **not** re-derive [R210](R210-decision-support-systems.md)'s taxonomy or its automation-bias
citation (assumed established there) and does **not** cover the reasoning algorithm a level-3
feature's recommendation would be generated by ([R506](R506-machine-reasoning.md)'s job) or
autonomous *planning* mechanics ([R507](R507-autonomous-planning-systems.md)'s job) — this topic is
about what the feature is allowed to surface to the trainee, not how it computes what to surface.

## 3. Concepts

**Where DOM-008 §4's line falls on [R210](R210-decision-support-systems.md)'s spectrum.** [R210](R210-decision-support-systems.md)'s levels 1-2 (data, information) are
clearly within bounds for any future in-world advisor; level 3 (recommendation — "here is a candidate
action and why") is the explicit ceiling DOM-008 §4 permits; level 4 (the system decides) is
explicitly out of bounds — a future feature proposal should be checked against landing at level 3 or
below, never level 4.

**A decision aid that is formally "advisory" can function as a de facto decision-maker in a
time-pressured military setting.** [Cummings (2004)](https://arc.aiaa.org/doi/10.2514/6.2004-6313)
analyzes exactly this failure using the 2003 U.S. Patriot air-defense-battery fratricide incidents
during Operation Iraqi Freedom as the motivating case: Patriot's engagement-recommendation system was
formally advisory (a human operator retained the fire decision), but time pressure and interface
design pushed operators toward accepting the system's classification with minimal independent
verification, contributing to at least one confirmed friendly-fire shootdown. Cummings frames this as
automation bias operating specifically in an "intelligent time-critical decision support system" —
precisely the category a future in-world combat-relevant advisor (e.g. an engagement-window or
custody-fusion suggestion tool) would belong to. The lesson for this simulator is that a feature's
*formal* decision-support level (per [R210](R210-decision-support-systems.md)) is not sufficient on its own; interface design and
time pressure can push a formally level-3 feature toward functioning like level 4 in practice.

**The difference between surfacing a recommendation and surfacing reasoning.** A level-3 advisor can
either present a bare recommendation ("jam asset X") or present the reasoning behind it ("asset X's
custody confidence is below the weapons-quality threshold and a jam window opens in 4 minutes") —
the latter is doctrinally preferable per [R210](R210-decision-support-systems.md)'s automation-bias concern (and the Patriot
case above, where operators had limited insight into *why* the system classified a track as hostile),
since it gives the trainee material to evaluate and potentially reject the recommendation rather than
just a conclusion to accept or decline.

**An advisor that only ever recommends the "correct" doctrinal action undermines the exercise's
educational purpose.** Per MSTR-003's plan-execute-as-the-unit-of-learning framing, a trainee who
always defers to an always-correct advisor never practices the judgment the exercise exists to train
— a future advisor feature should be evaluated against whether it preserves a genuine decision for
the trainee to make, not just technically avoid "deciding" while functionally removing the judgment
task. This is a training-specific concern beyond [R210](R210-decision-support-systems.md)'s general operational-decision-aid framing:
an operational Patriot-style system's goal is correct decisions as fast as possible, while this
simulator's goal is the trainee's judgment development, so a feature can be safe by Patriot's
standard (accurate, fast) and still fail this simulator's standard (removes the judgment task).

**Explainability as a requirement, not a nice-to-have.** A recommendation without legible reasoning
is functionally closer to a level-4 decision than its formal level-3 classification suggests, since
an opaque recommendation invites uncritical acceptance (automation bias, [R210](R210-decision-support-systems.md); the Patriot case
above) precisely because the trainee has no basis to evaluate it — any future advisor feature should
be required to expose its reasoning, not just its conclusion.

## 4. Operational Context

AI decision-support systems in real military and aviation contexts have repeatedly run into exactly
this tension — a recommendation system technically classified as "advisory" that in practice
functions as a decision-maker because operators uncritically accept its output under time pressure,
as documented in the 2003 Patriot fratricide analysis above — which is precisely why DOM-008 §4's
"advisor, not decider" framing and this topic's explainability requirement matter as design
constraints, not just labels.

## 5. Implementation Guidance

- **Any future in-world AI decision-support feature must cap itself at [R210](R210-decision-support-systems.md)'s level 3
  (recommendation), never level 4 (decision)**, per DOM-008 §4 — and should expose its underlying
  reasoning, not just a bare recommendation, per the explainability concern above.
- **Design the interface to slow down, not speed up, acceptance of a recommendation under time
  pressure** — per the Cummings/Patriot finding (§3), a level-3 feature's formal safety can be
  defeated by an interface that makes acceptance the path of least resistance during a time-critical
  decision; a future engagement-window or custody-fusion advisor should require the trainee to view
  the underlying reasoning before the recommended action becomes available to issue, not just display
  it alongside a one-click "accept."
- **Evaluate any proposed advisor feature against whether it preserves a genuine judgment task for
  the trainee** — per MSTR-003's plan-execute learning model, an advisor that is always right and
  always deferred to has defeated the exercise's pedagogical purpose even while staying formally
  within DOM-008 §4's letter.
- **Treat an opaque, unexplained recommendation as a design smell** — even if formally level 3, an
  unexplained recommendation functions like automation-bias bait and should be redesigned to expose
  its reasoning before being built.
