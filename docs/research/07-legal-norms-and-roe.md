# Legal, Normative & Rules-of-Engagement Frame

[← Research index](INDEX.md)

The simulator's vignettes encode ROE notes, escalation weights, attribution
scoring, and political consequences. This module grounds the *why*: the
treaty-law, customary-norm, and policy frame that explains which actions
are penalized, which are tolerated, and which are publicly off-limits.

For a PME wargame this is not legal advice — it is enough background that
operators can argue ROE choices at the table and White Cell can score
"responsible" play with a defensible standard.

## 1. The treaty floor

### Outer Space Treaty (1967)
The foundational instrument; near-universal ratification. Key articles for the sim:

- **Article I** — outer space is the "province of all mankind," free for use
  and exploration by all States. Forms the baseline against which *denial* of
  the regime — by debris, by sustained EW, or by indiscriminate weapons — is
  judged as harmful.
- **Article II** — no national appropriation of outer space or celestial
  bodies. Disposes of "occupy the orbital slot" as a winning condition; orbital
  slots are coordinated through the ITU, not seized.
- **Article IV** — no nuclear or other weapons of mass destruction in orbit,
  on celestial bodies, or stationed in outer space. **This is the legal
  prohibition that makes the v1-excluded orbital nuclear ASAT
  (`03-counterspace-taxonomy.md` § "out-of-scope") not just a balancing problem
  but a treaty violation.**
- **Article VI** — States bear international responsibility for **national
  activities**, governmental *or* non-governmental. A commercial operator's
  satellite is the launching State's legal responsibility — relevant when a
  Red Cell jams a Western SATCOM operator's commercial bird.
- **Article VII** — international liability for damage caused by space objects.
  Codified by the 1972 **Liability Convention**: a State is liable for damage
  its space objects cause on Earth (absolute liability) or to other space
  objects (fault liability).
- **Article IX** — "due regard" to the corresponding interests of other
  States; consultations required where an activity may cause "potentially
  harmful interference." The norms case against persistent close-approach RPO
  often rests here.

### Registration Convention (1976) and Liability Convention (1972)
Together with the OST, these set the public-record expectation
(register your space objects with the UN) and the liability flow for damage,
including damage from debris generated by destructive ASAT tests.

## 2. The law of armed conflict (LOAC) applied to space

Outside the OST's peacetime frame, an *armed conflict* in space is governed by
the same LOAC principles as any other domain. The principles most cited in
official US, UK, French, and NATO policy on space operations:

- **Distinction** — targets must be military objectives, not civilian objects.
  Dual-use systems (commercial SATCOM, GNSS for civilian aviation) make this
  hard; the sim's "ambiguity / commercial dependence" wrinkle (e.g., Vignette 2
  / SATCOM cyber & link) maps directly onto this.
- **Proportionality** — collateral damage must not be excessive vs. concrete
  and direct military advantage. **Debris is the textbook proportionality
  problem in space**: a kinetic kill against one satellite that denies the
  orbital regime to all users for years is the canonical disproportionate
  attack.
- **Precautions** — feasible measures to minimize incidental harm. Reversible
  effects (cyber, EW, RPO escort) generally satisfy precaution better than
  kinetic; this is why the sim's five-D's ladder treats them as lower-
  escalation.
- **Military necessity** — actions must contribute to military advantage. A
  destructive ASAT test in peacetime, with no military objective, is hard to
  defend on necessity even before the proportionality argument.

The **2009 Tallinn Manual** (cyber LOAC) and its **Manual on International
Law Applicable to Military Uses of Outer Space (MILAMOS, 2022)** and the
**Woomera Manual (2023)** are the leading academic codifications. They are
not binding law, but they describe how States *say* the law applies and are
widely used in coalition training.

## 3. The 2022 destructive-DA-ASAT test moratorium

The single most important normative development of the post-Nudol-2021 era,
directly load-bearing on the sim's "responsible play" scoring.

- **18 April 2022** — US Vice President announces a **US moratorium on
  destructive direct-ascent ASAT tests**, citing the debris from the Nov-2021
  Russian Nudol test that destroyed Tselina-D and threatened the ISS.
- By the end of 2024 the moratorium had been joined by **>30 States** including
  the UK, France, Germany, Japan, Canada, Australia, South Korea, and most of
  NATO. **Russia, China, India, and Iran have not joined.**
- **UNGA Resolution 77/41 (Dec 2022)** translated the commitment into a
  political declaration, passed 155–9.

**Sim implication.** A Red Cell that uses a debris-generating kinetic ASAT
incurs UN-condemnation injects and a political-cost spike in Vignette 5
because that move is *also* a norms violation as of 2022, not just because
debris is bad for both sides. White Cell facilitators should explain this on
the after-action review.

## 4. Norms and confidence-building measures still under negotiation

- **UN OEWG on reducing space threats through norms** (UNGA 76/231, 2021–2023)
  — proposed norms such as "no harmful interference," restrictions on close
  proximity operations, and pre-notification of high-risk maneuvers. No
  binding outcome, but the working group's reports are a useful source of
  draft norm language.
- **Common-but-non-binding "rules of the road":** no destructive ASAT tests,
  pre-notification of conjunctions, transparency on rendezvous and proximity
  operations (RPO), restraint on persistent shadowing of high-value assets.
  These are the standards a Blue Cell can plausibly invoke when calling out
  Red behavior, and the standards Red can be scored against in AAR.
- **Commercial / non-State norms:** the Space Safety Coalition's *Best
  Practices for the Sustainability of Space Operations*; the OneWeb / SpaceX
  collision-avoidance interoperability standards. Relevant when commercial
  operators are part of a scenario (Vignette 2, MS-03 SSA coalition).

## 5. ROE design pattern (how the sim maps law and norms to play)

Each vignette's `intro_brief.{cell}.roe_note` field codifies the legal/normative
state. The pattern:

| Layer | What it gates | Sim primitive |
|---|---|---|
| **Treaty law** | Nuclear-in-orbit (OST Art. IV) | excluded from effect catalog (see `03-counterspace-taxonomy.md`) |
| **LOAC distinction/proportionality** | Kinetic ASAT, attacks on dual-use assets | `red_kinetic_authorized` parameter (OFF by default in most vignettes); debris generation triggers escalation injects |
| **2022 moratorium / norms** | Destructive ASAT testing in peacetime | UN-condemnation inject template; political-cost scoring |
| **National ROE / mission tasking** | Whether your cell has been authorized | `roe_note` field, gates which orders the cell may *issue* even if the effect category is in catalog |
| **Coalition / partner constraints** | What allied SDA-feeds will share | per-vignette friendly-forces composition |

> **Teaching note (White Cell):** the lesson is that "Can I do this?" decomposes
> into four questions — (a) is it permitted by treaty? (b) does it satisfy LOAC?
> (c) does it cross a public norm whose violation will cost you politically?
> (d) has my own chain of command authorized me? Most counterspace decisions
> bind on (c) and (d) long before (a) or (b).
